Summary

Patrick Cahill was born in 1894 to Bridget Flanagan & Patrick Cahill. In 1895, his sister Mary Cahill was born. When Patrick was just 3, his father died. Bridget Flanagan then remarried Denis Hayes and had a further 9 children.

Patrick emigrated to the US in 1912 when he was just 17. In 1913 he made his way to Canada (possibly to his uncle Tom Flanagan in Alberta), and enlisted in the Canadian Army in 1915. In April 1916 he was sent to England and on to France to fight in WW1.

On April 9th, 1917 (Easter Monday) he was part of the Canadian regiment that fought in the Battle of Vimy Ridge and sadly, Patrick didn't survive that day. He was killed in action and is buried in a British military cemetery in France. He was just 23 when he died.

Battle of Vimy Ridge (April 9th, 1917 - April 12th, 1917)

The Battle of Vimy Ridge (9â€“12 April 1917) was a defining First World War engagement in northern France where the Canadian Corps captured a strategically vital, heavily fortified ridge from the German 6th Army. It is widely considered a "coming of age" moment for Canada, as it was the first time all four divisions of the Canadian Corps fought together as a single formation.

Key Details of the Battle

  • Date: 9 April (Easter Monday) to 12 April 1917.
  • Location: Vimy Ridge, near Arras, Franceâ€”a 7-km long escarpment overlooking the Douai Plain.
  • Commanders: Led by British General Sir Julian Byng and Canadian Major-General Arthur Currie.
  • Result: A decisive Allied victory that captured the ridge, forcing a German retreat of three kilometres.
  • Casualties: The victory came at a heavy cost of 10,602 Canadian casualties, including 3,598 fatalities.

Innovative Tactics & Preparation

The success at Vimy is attributed to unprecedented meticulous planning and technical innovation:

  • Creeping Barrage: Infantry advanced in precise 100-yard increments immediately behind a moving wall of artillery fire, catching Germans before they could leave their bunkers.
  • Tunneling (Subways): Extensive underground networks allowed thousands of troops to move to the front lines safely and unseen.
  • Intelligence for All: For the first time, detailed maps and objectives were distributed to every soldier, not just officers, so the advance could continue even if leaders were hit.
  • "Week of Suffering": A massive preliminary bombardment of over one million shells "softened" German defences for a week before the attack.

The creeping barrage was a "wall of fire" created by hundreds of artillery pieces that moved forward in timed increments, allowing infantry to advance safely behind it. This tactic replaced the ineffective strategy of long, stationary bombardments, which often gave defenders enough time to man their machine guns once the shelling stopped.

How it Worked at Vimy Ridge

  • Precise Timing: The artillery was synchronized to "lift" its fire and move forward at a set paceâ€”at Vimy, this was typically 100 yards every 3 minutes.
  • Infantry "Leaning Into" the Barrage: Canadian soldiers were trained to stay as close as 50 to 80 yards behind the falling shells. This was dangerous but essential; it ensured they reached German trenches before the defenders could emerge from their deep underground bunkers.
  • The "Shield" Effect: The barrage didn't just kill; it suppressed the enemy with noise, dust, and smoke, effectively acting as a moving shield for the advancing Canadians.
  • Open-Loop Coordination: Because there were no portable radios for real-time adjustments, the entire operation relied on a strict prearranged schedule. Soldiers carried detailed maps showing exactly where the barrage would be at specific times.

The Canadian attack plan at Vimy Ridge used four color-coded objective linesâ€”Black, Red, Blue, and Brownâ€”to coordinate the advance of 100,000 soldiers across the 7-kilometre front. This "phase line" system allowed the Canadian Corps to maintain momentum by "leapfrogging" fresh troops through those who had already captured earlier targets.

The Four Objective Lines

  • The Black Line: The first objective, representing the German forward defensive positions. All four divisions reached this line within approximately 35 to 60 minutes of the 5:30 a.m. "zero hour."
  • The Red Line: The critical second objective. It included Hill 145 (the ridge's highest point and current site of the memorial) and the "Pimple" (a fortified knoll). Capturing this line gave the Canadians control of the ridge's crest.
  • The Blue Line: The third objective, which involved capturing the village of ThÃ©lus and the woods overlooking the Douai Plain. Only the 1st and 2nd Divisions (on the southern, wider part of the front) were tasked with reaching this and the final line.
  • The Brown Line: The final objective and the German second main defensive line. It was located roughly 4,000 yards from the original start line. By 2:00 p.m. on the first day, the 1st and 2nd Divisions had secured this line, effectively completing their primary mission.

The "Leapfrog" Tactic

To prevent soldiers from becoming too exhausted to defend their gains, the Vimy plan used a staggered approach:

  1. Initial Wave: Captured and consolidated the Black Line.
  2. Support Wave: Passed through the first group to assault the Red Line.
  3. Reserve Wave: Fresh brigades then "leapfrogged" both groups to push toward the Blue and Brown Lines.

This was all timed to the creeping barrage. For example, the artillery would pause for a set amount of time at the Black Line to allow the second wave of infantry to move up and prepare for the next "lift" toward the Red Line.

National Legacy

The battle is often called the "birth of a nation" for Canada. Following the victory, Arthur Currie was knighted and became the first Canadian to command the Canadian Corps. Today, the Canadian National Vimy Memorial stands on Hill 145, the ridge's highest point, on land granted to Canada in perpetuity by France. It is inscribed with the names of 11,285 Canadians killed in France with no known grave.
